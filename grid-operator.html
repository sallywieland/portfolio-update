Case Study · Native App · Energy Infrastructure

Designing for a system that already existed.

MISO had 20 years of desktop tools, an established brand, and a user base of energy professionals who knew their domain cold. The challenge wasn't to reimagine — it was to adapt without disrupting what already worked.

Client

Midcontinent Independent System Operator (MISO)

Platform

Native iOS + Android

Role

Sole designer, greenfield to App Store

Outcome

5.0 App Store · led to misoenergy.org redesign

The context

First project with a client who runs the grid for 42 million people.

MISO manages the transmission of electricity across 15 states and Manitoba — roughly 186,000 MW of market capacity, 72,000 miles of transmission lines, and a wholesale market that settles around $2 billion a month. Their existing tools were desktop-only, built for control rooms, and deeply embedded in how their users worked.

This was Crafted's first project with MISO — a greenfield native app, but not a blank slate. The brand, the data structures, the domain vocabulary: all of it already existed. The job was to extend the system into a new context, not replace it.

The challenge

Same data. New context. No blank slate.

The desktop tools had deep information density — grids, charts, maps with dozens of labeled data points. Bringing that to mobile meant working within every constraint at once: screen size, touch targets, connection speeds, and an audience that had been using the desktop version for years.

The solution wasn't to simplify the data — it was to make it configurable. A Dashboard Settings screen lets users toggle modules on or off and drag to reorder. The app adapts to each person's workflow without changing what the system actually contains.

Dashboard Settings — toggle visibility, drag to reorder

Design decision

The constraint made it better than the original.

The desktop LMP Contour Map showed every hub label floating over the territory at once — workable on a large monitor, unreadable on a phone. Shrinking it wasn't an option. So the map stayed clean, and a user's favorited hubs moved to a persistent bottom card with 24-hour sparklines.

The desktop version didn't have sparklines at all. Working within the limits of mobile forced a structural change that added information the original never had.

What I paid attention to

How you work within a system.

Keep the vocabulary intact

LMP, ACE, ARR/FTR, LZN — the app uses the terms its users already know. There was no impulse to simplify the language for a general audience. Changing established terminology in a professional tool doesn't feel friendlier; it just creates confusion and erodes trust.

Extend the brand, don't replace it

MISO had an established visual identity built up over years. The app needed to feel native to that system — not like a redesign layered on top of it. Modernizing the brand meant refining what was already there, not introducing a new aesthetic that the desktop tools wouldn't match.

Personalization that follows the data

The hubs you star in Dashboard Settings appear as starred markers on the LMP Map. Notification preferences carry through to the notifications screen. The configurability isn't a settings page bolted on — it threads through the whole app, because that's how the underlying data model works.

Dark mode as operational context

Control room environments, field conditions, early-morning shift checks — these are real use contexts for this tool. Dark mode wasn't added as a preference toggle. It was treated as a legitimate part of how the app gets used and designed in from the beginning.

Outcome

5 stars. Then they asked us back for the website.

MISO Mobile launched to a 5.0 rating on the App Store. The app continues to ship new features — GI Interactive Queue, Carbon Emission Mix, Dashboard LMP Hubs — as the engagement deepened. Working within the existing system so carefully is what made the follow-on possible: MISO brought Crafted back to redesign misoenergy.org.
